01 · The Opportunity

Pre-loved is the part of luxury that is actually growing.

South Africa's luxury market is expanding while the rest of retail stalls, and the second hand end of it is growing fastest of all. Leather bags sit right at the centre of that shift.

R93bn
Projected SA luxury goods sales by 2030, on roughly 9% annual growth (Euromonitor).
+27.9%

+14.6%

$360bn
Where the global resale market is heading by 2030, up from about $210bn, growing near 10% a year (BCG).

Three forces line up at once. First, a cost of living squeeze means buyers who still want the label now shop pre-loved without embarrassment. Second, sustainability has made a second hand Chanel a smart choice rather than a compromise. Third, resale value in South Africa is genuinely strong. A Chanel Classic Flap holds over 64% of its retail value, and real listed examples include a Louis Vuitton Monogram Orfevre at R49,000 (about 73% of retail) and a Saint Laurent Sac de Jour at R40,000 (about 74%). This is a category where good stock sells, and sells for real money.

02 · The Honest Teardown

Where this can go wrong, said plainly.

A proposal that only lists the upside is a sales pitch, not a plan. Here are the three real risks in this business, and how the model below is built specifically to remove them.

1. The fakes problem is real, and it is expensive.

The super fake market is now good enough to fool a casual eye. Sell one convincingly fake bag and the brand's reputation, and possibly its bank account, is gone. This is the single biggest risk in pre-loved luxury, full stop.

How we kill it: every bag is authenticated before it is ever listed, using a paid authentication service (Entrupy style AI plus human review, roughly R450 to R900 per bag). Nothing goes live on a guess.

2. Buying stock outright ties up cash in things that might be fake or might not sell.

The classic beginner mistake is spending R200,000 buying bags to resell. If a few are fake, or slow to move, that capital is dead. For a new brand this is how you lose money fast.

How we kill it: we do not buy stock. We sell other people's bags on consignment and take a commission. Zero inventory cash, zero fake bag capital risk. This is the heart of the whole plan (see The Model).

3. After fees, margins are thinner than they look.

Between authentication, courier, payment processing and the odd return, the gap between the sticker price and the money that reaches your pocket is real. Anyone promising huge easy margins is not being straight with you.

How we handle it: the forecast further down is built bottom up with every one of those costs subtracted, so the net profit figure is the money that actually lands, not the headline.

03 · The Model

Consignment. We sell on behalf, and take a cut.

Owners of beautiful bags hand them to HAUS OF PATINA. We authenticate, photograph, list, market and sell them, then pay the owner their share and keep a commission. No bag is ever bought. No cash is ever at risk.

Why it wins

Zero capital risk

We never spend money owning stock. If a bag does not sell, we have lost nothing but a little time, and the AI does the time.

Why it wins

Fakes cannot bankrupt us

Because we do not buy, a rejected fake simply goes back to its owner. We authenticate before listing, so it never reaches a buyer.

Why it wins

Endless supply

Every wardrobe in Bishopscourt, Umhlanga and Sandton is potential stock. We unlock it without buying it.

Our commission structure

Global consignment sits between 15% and 30%. The RealReal keeps 20% to 30% depending on value, Fashionphile takes 30% on the first $3,000 and less above that. We propose a tiered commission that is competitive for high value consignors while protecting margin on smaller pieces.

The one thing a human must physically do, and how it stays off your plate.

A bag has to be physically inspected, authenticated and packed. That is the only step software cannot touch. It does not land on you. It is fully outsourced:

Paid authenticationA specialist service verifies each bag (roughly R450 to R900 per item), so the genuineness call is never a guess and never yours.
Courier pickupThe Courier Guy collects from the owner and delivers to the buyer (national parcels from about R90 to R200). No trips, no post office queues.
A part-time VAAn affordable SA virtual assistant (roughly R150 to R260 an hour, a few hours a week) handles the physical intake, checks and packing.

Your total involvement: approving the odd big decision and enjoying the brand you already built. That is the design goal of this entire proposal.
